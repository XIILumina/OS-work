# A Boring Tuesday

*A short story featuring XIII "Lumina"*

---

Tuesday.

I think it's Tuesday. Could be Wednesday. The days blur together when you don't sleep, don't eat, don't *need* anything except something—*anything*—to break the monotony.

I'm standing on a rooftop somewhere in the city. Don't know which city. Don't particularly care. They all look the same after a while: concrete and glass and tiny humans scurrying between them like ants who've convinced themselves they're building something important.

I crack my neck. Once to the left. Twice to the right.

A maintenance worker on the adjacent building drops his wrench. It clatters down six stories. He's staring at me—at my silhouette against the sunset. Probably can't see my face from that distance, but the height alone is enough. Two meters of *wrong* standing where nothing should be.

I wave.

He runs.

"Sorry," I call after him, knowing he can't hear me. Knowing he wouldn't care if he could.

I do feel bad, sometimes. Scaring people is fun when they're comfortable enough to laugh about it later. When they run? When genuine terror replaces the brief thrill of surprise? 

It gets old.

Everything gets old when you have eternity and nothing to fill it with.

---

I drop from the rooftop—seven stories, straight down—and land in the alley with enough force to crack the pavement. My boots take the impact without complaint. They're good boots. Heavy. Steel-reinforced. Built for exactly this kind of stupid.

A cat yowls and bolts.

"Yeah, yeah. I'm scary. Tell your friends."

The alley empties into a main street. Evening foot traffic. Commuters heading home, students heading to bars, the usual ebb and flow of human routine. I pull my coat tighter and tuck my chin into my turtleneck, letting my front hair fall over my eyes.

It doesn't really hide what I am. The black liquid still seeps from the corners of my eyes, trailing down my cheeks in thin rivulets. My grin—permanent, too wide, full of teeth that are just slightly too sharp—doesn't exactly scream *human*.

But people don't *look* anymore. Not really. They see the coat, the boots, the general shape of a woman, and their brains fill in the rest. *Goth girl. Weird makeup. Cosplay, maybe.*

Close enough.

I walk.

No destination. No plan. Just... movement. The illusion of purpose.

A street performer is playing violin on the corner—some classical piece I don't recognize. I stop and listen for a moment. She's good. Actually good, not just *good for a street performer*. Her eyes are closed, lost in the music.

I wonder what that feels like. To lose yourself in something.

I drop a gold coin into her case—I made it this morning out of boredom, compressed matter and rearranged electrons—and keep walking before she opens her eyes.

---

Three blocks later, I hear it.

Shouting. Muffled, coming from below street level. Basement or underground garage, maybe.

I stop. Tilt my head.

More shouting. A woman's voice, young. Scared. Then a man's voice—older, angry—cutting her off.

I feel the faintest flicker of interest.

*Finally.*

I follow the sound to a service entrance half-hidden between two buildings. The door's locked, but locks are just matter arranged in inconvenient ways. I rest my palm against it—the cube hums beneath my skin—and the metal shivers, melts, reforms into something that isn't a lock anymore.

The door swings open.

Stairs leading down. Dim lighting. The smell of oil and old concrete.

I descend.

---

It's a parking garage. Private, from the looks of it. Three black vans, expensive. A dozen men, some armed. And in the center: four women, hands zip-tied, sitting on the floor with hoods over their heads.

Trafficking operation. 

Classic.

I watch from the shadows for a moment. The men are discussing logistics—pickup times, routes, prices. Talking about people like they're cargo.

I crack my neck again.

One of the men—shaved head, neck tattoos, holding a pistol like he knows how to use it—stops mid-sentence and looks in my direction.

"The fuck was that?"

I step into the light.

Twelve pairs of eyes lock onto me.

Silence.

Then one of them laughs. "The hell are you supposed to be? Halloween was three months ago, freak."

I smile wider. Not that I have a choice—my grin doesn't exactly turn off—but I put effort into it. Let them see the teeth.

The laughter dies.

"Gentlemen," I say, my voice layered and echoing in the enclosed space, "you're making me regret coming down here. I was hoping for something *interesting*."

Tattoo-neck raises his gun. "Get the fuck out or I'll—"

I'm across the garage before he finishes the sentence.

My hand—still human, for now—closes around his wrist. I squeeze.

Bones snap like dry twigs.

He screams.

I let him drop and turn to the others. "So. Who's next?"

---

They open fire.

Predictable.

Bullets punch through my coat, my turtleneck, my skin. I feel each impact—sharp, annoying—but they don't *hurt*. Not really. Pain is just information, and I have infinite bandwidth.

I let them empty their magazines. Let them see the holes tear through me, the black ichor leaking out instead of blood, the way the wounds close almost as fast as they open.

When the clicking of empty chambers fills the garage, I sigh.

"Disappointing."

My hands shift. Bone pushes through skin at the wrists—white and gleaming, elongating into curved claws. I flex my fingers and the claws extend further, ten crescent blades sharp enough to split atoms.

"Now," I say, "let's try this again."

I move.

Not fast by my standards. But to them? I'm a blur.

The first man goes down in two swipes—one across the chest, one through the throat. Clean. Efficient.

The second tries to run. I hook my claw into his ankle and pull. He hits the ground hard. I don't give him time to scream.

The third pulls a knife. Adorable. I catch the blade between two claws and snap it, then drive my hand through his sternum. The cube hums, hungry, and his body collapses inward—flesh and bone and blood drawn into me, absorbed, *stored*.

The rest break.

Some run for the vans. Some for the stairs. One just stands there, frozen, pissing himself.

I let the runners go. They won't get far, and honestly? I'm not doing this for them.

I'm doing it because I'm *bored*.

---

The frozen one—I walk up to him slowly. Let my claws retract, let my hands return to something almost human.

"Hey," I say gently. "Relax. I'm not going to kill you."

He stares at me, trembling.

"I'm going to give you a choice." I crouch down to his eye level. The black liquid drips from my chin onto the concrete. "You can walk out of here. Right now. Leave this life. Never touch another person like this again."

He nods frantically.

"Or," I continue, my grin widening, "you can try to run, and I'll hunt you down for sport. Your call."

He runs.

I laugh. Can't help it.

Then I turn to the women.

---

They're still hooded, but I can hear their breathing—rapid, panicked. They heard everything.

I kneel beside the nearest one and carefully pull the hood off.

She flinches. Eyes squeezed shut.

"Hey," I say softly. Layered voices make softness hard, but I try. "It's over. You're safe."

She opens her eyes.

Sees me.

Screams.

I sigh.

"Right. Yeah. I get it."

I use a claw to cut the zip-ties—careful, precise—and do the same for the others. Then I step back, giving them space.

"Stairs are that way," I say, pointing. "Police will probably be here soon. Someone definitely heard the gunshots."

They scramble to their feet and run.

None of them look back.

I don't take it personally.

---

I'm alone again.

The garage is quiet except for the hum of fluorescent lights and the slow drip of black ichor from my wounds—still closing, still healing.

I look down at the bodies. Three dead. One absorbed. The rest fled.

I feel... nothing.

No satisfaction. No guilt. No sense of accomplishment.

Just the same hollow nothing that I woke up with this morning.

I crack my neck again.

"Boring," I mutter.

The cube pulses beneath my skin, warm and alive, the weight of a billion consumed souls pressing against the edges of my consciousness. They whisper sometimes—incoherent, endless—but I've learned to tune them out.

I wonder, not for the first time, what I'm even doing.

Protecting humanity? Sure. In the abstract. Humanity as a *concept* is fine. Interesting, even.

But individual humans?

They run. They scream. They see me and all they know is *monster*.

And maybe they're right.

---

I leave through the front entrance this time. The door's locked, but I melt it.

Outside, the city's still moving. Still alive. Sirens in the distance, growing closer.

I pull my hood up and walk.

No destination.

No plan.

Just movement.

The illusion of purpose.

---

Somewhere across the city, a woman gets home safely. Four women, actually. They'll have nightmares about tonight—about the men, about the garage, about the *thing* that saved them.

But they'll be alive.

And I'll still be here.

Wandering.

Waiting.

Searching for something I can't name.

*Maybe tomorrow will be different,* I think.

But I know it won't be.

It never is.

---

**END**

*Author's Note: This story explores Lumina's perspective during a "typical" night. Despite her overwhelming power, her greatest enemy isn't a rival or a threat—it's the crushing weight of eternal boredom and a search for meaning in a world that fears her.
